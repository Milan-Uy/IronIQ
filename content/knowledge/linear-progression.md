## Linear Progression for Novice Lifters

Linear progression (LP) is the simplest and most effective method of programming strength for beginners. The core premise: add weight to the bar every single session. If you squatted 135 lbs on Monday, you squat 140 lbs on Wednesday, 145 lbs on Friday. Repeat until you can't.

### Why LP Works for Novices

Novice lifters — anyone who cannot complete a planned session with a given load — have not yet developed the neurological efficiency or training history that makes their adaptation more complex. A beginner's recovery window is short: they can stress, recover, and adapt within 48–72 hours. This means the optimal training frequency is every-other-day, and the stress-recovery-adaptation cycle completes within a single session cycle. More advanced lifters need days or weeks to complete the same cycle.

LP exploits this novice window aggressively. Gains that take an advanced lifter a year to accumulate can be made in months by a consistent novice using LP.

### Standard LP Structure

The classic LP setup (as in Starting Strength and StrongLifts 5×5):

- **Frequency:** 3 days per week, alternating A and B workouts
- **Sets/reps:** 3 sets of 5 (3×5) or 5 sets of 5 (5×5)
- **Increment:** 5 lbs per session for upper body lifts; 10 lbs per session for lower body lifts (beginners can handle larger jumps initially)
- **Core lifts:** Squat every session; press and deadlift alternate or share sessions

**Example StrongLifts 5×5 Week:**
- Monday A: Squat 5×5, Bench 5×5, Barbell Row 5×5
- Wednesday B: Squat 5×5, Overhead Press 5×5, Deadlift 1×5
- Friday A: Repeat A

### When LP Ends

LP ends when you can no longer add weight every session — you miss reps, stall for 3 consecutive sessions, or begin failing lifts. This is not a failure; it is a natural transition. The novice phase typically lasts 3–6 months for a complete beginner, shorter for someone returning to lifting after a break.

Signs the LP is ending:
- Missing the top set on 2–3 consecutive sessions despite adequate sleep and nutrition
- Recovery taking longer than 48 hours
- Sessions requiring more warm-up sets to hit working weights

### Troubleshooting Stalls

Before declaring LP over, check:
1. **Sleep:** 7–9 hours per night is non-negotiable for LP recovery.
2. **Caloric intake:** LP requires a caloric surplus or at minimum maintenance. Aggressive cuts kill LP.
3. **Technique:** A form breakdown often looks like a strength plateau. Have someone review your lifts.
4. **Stress:** Work, life, and illness load affects recovery capacity.

### Resetting

When stalling, a 10% load reset and rebuilding with proper LP increment size often extends the novice phase by several more weeks. Do not be too proud to reset.

### What Comes After LP

Once LP is exhausted, the lifter transitions to intermediate programming: Texas Method, GZCLP, or early intermediate strength programs where the progression unit becomes a week (adding weight weekly) rather than a session. This is normal and expected progression.

LP is not a long-term program — it is a fast-pass through beginner gains. Use it, extract every pound of progress from it, and move on when it's done.
