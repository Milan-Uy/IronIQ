## RPE and RIR: Auto-Regulating Training Intensity

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) and RIR (Reps in Reserve) are tools for quantifying training intensity based on how hard a set actually feels — rather than using fixed percentages of a one-rep max. They allow training to respond to the lifter's day-to-day readiness, making programs more resilient to life's variability.

### The RPE Scale

The standard RPE scale in strength training (adapted from Borg and popularized in powerlifting by Mike Tuchscherer of RTS):

| RPE | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 10 | Maximum effort — could not have done another rep |
| 9 | Could have done 1 more rep |
| 8 | Could have done 2 more reps |
| 7 | Could have done 3 more reps |
| 6 | Could have done 4 more reps |
| 5 and below | Warm-up territory; multiple reps in reserve |

RPE 7–9 is where most productive training occurs. RPE 10 (true maximal effort) is reserved for competition, testing, or planned max-effort sessions — not every working set.

### RIR (Reps in Reserve)

RIR is simply the inverse expression of the same concept. An RPE 8 = 2 RIR (two reps remaining before failure). An RPE 9 = 1 RIR. RIR is often more intuitive for beginners because it answers the question directly: "How many more reps could you have done?"

RIR-based training (popularized by Eric Helms and the RP/3DMJ approach) prescribes sets as "leave 2 reps in reserve" (2 RIR = RPE 8) rather than a percentage.

### Why Use RPE/RIR Instead of Percentages?

**Percentage limitations:**
- A "75% of 1RM" day on a poor-sleep, high-stress day may feel like 85%. The lifter either overtaxes themselves to hit the number, or bails on perfectly good training because they feel bad.
- 1RM values fluctuate — a tested 1RM from 8 weeks ago may not reflect current strength.
- Percentages require knowing your 1RM, which may not be applicable in hypertrophy training with higher rep ranges.

**RPE/RIR advantages:**
- Adapts to daily readiness — a 3 RIR set is a 3 RIR set whether you're peaking or tired.
- Requires no 1RM test; useful across all rep ranges.
- Teaches lifters to read their own bodies, building long-term self-coaching skill.
- Flexible: if you're strong that day, you use more weight; if fatigued, less — the stimulus remains consistent.

### Practical Application

A program might prescribe: "Squat 4×4 @ RPE 8." The lifter chooses a weight they estimate they could do for 6 reps (4 reps + 2 in reserve = RPE 8). They adjust set to set based on how the weight feels.

For beginners, RPE calibration takes time — most novices underestimate how many reps they have left. A common drill: predict your RIR before finishing a set, then do more reps to verify. Repeat until prediction accuracy improves.

### Limitations

- RPE is subjective; two lifters at "RPE 8" may have different actual proximity to failure.
- Beginners often have poor RPE calibration — they are not yet accurately able to perceive proximity to failure.
- RPE works best as a secondary tool layered onto structured programming, not as the sole basis for loading decisions.

### RPE-Based Programming Structures

- **Max effort (ME) work:** Top set at RPE 9-10, then percentage back-off sets
- **Volume work:** Multiple sets at RPE 7-8 (2-3 RIR), focusing on accumulating tonnage
- **Deload:** Same movements at RPE 5-6 (4+ RIR), reduced volume

Combining RPE targets with prescribed rep ranges (e.g., "3×8 @ RPE 7") gives a clear and flexible prescription that accommodates daily readiness without sacrificing training structure.
