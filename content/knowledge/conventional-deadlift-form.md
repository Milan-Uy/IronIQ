## Conventional Deadlift Technique

The conventional deadlift is the most fundamental pulling movement in strength training. It trains the entire posterior chain — hamstrings, glutes, spinal erectors — along with the upper back, traps, and grip. When done correctly it is one of the safest and most productive exercises possible.

### Setup: The Five-Point Checklist

Before pulling, establish this setup:

1. **Bar over mid-foot:** The bar should be about 1 inch from the shins, directly over the mid-foot (not the toes). Looking from above, the barbell passes through the middle of the foot.
2. **Hip-width stance:** Feet roughly hip-width apart, toes pointed out 15–30 degrees.
3. **Grip just outside the legs:** The arms hang straight down from the shoulders — just outside the legs. Mixed grip (one overhand, one underhand) or hook grip for heavier loads.
4. **Shins to bar:** When you hinge down to grip, push your shins forward until they touch the bar. The bar does not move.
5. **Chest up, back flat:** Before pulling, take a big breath, brace the trunk, and "chest up" — this sets the lumbar curve in extension. The hips will be roughly at 45 degrees from horizontal, somewhere between a squat and a stiff-leg.

### Leg Drive vs. Hip Hinge

The deadlift is initiated by pushing the floor away (leg drive), not by pulling the bar up. Think of it as a leg press where the bar happens to be in your hands. This mental cue keeps the hips from shooting up early and ensures the quads contribute through the first half of the lift. Once the bar passes the knees, it becomes a hip hinge — drive the hips through to lockout.

### Bar Path

The bar travels in a straight vertical line. This is non-negotiable for efficiency. The bar drags up the shins and thighs — this is correct. If the bar swings away from the body, the moment arm on the lower back increases dramatically and the lift becomes much harder and riskier.

### Lat Engagement

"Protect your armpits" or "bend the bar around your legs" are cues to engage the lats throughout the pull. Active lats keep the bar close to the body and protect the lower back from flexion under load.

### Lockout

At the top, stand tall: hips fully extended, glutes squeezed, shoulders back. Do not hyperextend the lower back at lockout — it is not a back extension. The body is vertical, the bar is hanging at arm's length.

### Descent

The bar is lowered by hinging at the hips first — push the hips back until the bar passes the knees, then bend the knees to lower the bar to the floor. Maintain control; do not drop the bar unless using bumper plates in a designated area.

### Common Errors

- **Jerking the bar:** Pulling slack out of the bar with a sudden yank creates a bar oscillation and a jarring load on the spine. Instead, take the slack out slowly and increase tension progressively before the bar breaks the floor.
- **Rounding the lower back:** The most dangerous error. Usually caused by insufficient bracing, too much load, or poor setup. A neutral spine must be maintained throughout.
- **Hips shooting up first:** The hips rise faster than the shoulders, converting the movement into a stiff-leg deadlift with a rounded back. Cue: push the floor away simultaneously with the pull.
- **Bar drifting forward:** Engages the lower back excessively. Keep the bar on the body.
- **Hyperextending at lockout:** Unnecessary and risky; lock out by squeezing the glutes and standing tall, not by throwing the chest back.
