## Low-Bar Squat Technique

The low-bar squat places the barbell across the rear deltoids — roughly two inches below the top of the traps — rather than on the traps themselves. This position lowers the bar's center of mass, allowing a more forward lean of the torso and recruiting the hip extensors (glutes and hamstrings) to a greater degree than the high-bar variation.

### Bar Position

Set the bar below the spine of the scapula, resting on a shelf formed by the rear delts and the lower traps. Grip the bar as close to the shoulders as shoulder mobility allows — a narrow grip keeps the upper back tight and prevents the bar from rolling. Elbows should point back and slightly down, not straight back.

### Setup and Brace

Before unracking, take a full breath into the belly (Valsalva maneuver), brace the entire trunk hard — 360 degrees of pressure against your belt or waistband — and maintain this brace through the entire rep. Step back with two steps, no more. Feet should be roughly shoulder-width apart with toes pointed out 30–45 degrees.

### The Hip Hinge Pattern

Initiate the squat by pushing the knees out in the direction the toes point and simultaneously breaking at the hips. The hips travel back and down. The torso leans forward substantially — this is correct for low-bar and should not be confused with good-morning-ing the squat. The bar stays over the mid-foot at all times viewed from the side.

### Depth

Squat to below parallel: the hip crease must descend below the top of the knee. This ensures the full range of motion necessary for complete muscular development and hip mobility. Stopping above parallel leaves strength and muscle on the table and does not fully train the hip extensors through their range.

### Knee Tracking

Knees track over the toes throughout the movement. Drive knees out actively during the descent and especially during the ascent. Knees caving inward (valgus collapse) is the most common low-bar error and indicates weak hip abductors or insufficient cue awareness.

### The Ascent

Drive through the whole foot — not just the heel — and think "chest up" to prevent the hips from rising faster than the bar. If the hips shoot up while the chest stays low, the movement devolves into a good morning and the lower back takes over for the quads prematurely.

### Common Errors

- **Bar too high:** Slides up to the traps, converting it to an unintentional high-bar squat.
- **Knees caving:** Weak hip abductors or failure to push knees out.
- **Butt wink:** Excessive posterior pelvic tilt at depth, often from hip anatomy or inadequate hip mobility — a slight tuck is normal; severe rounding of the lumbar spine is not.
- **Losing the brace:** Breathing out mid-rep collapses trunk stability; breathe only at the top.
- **Heels rising:** Insufficient ankle mobility or weight shifted too far forward.
