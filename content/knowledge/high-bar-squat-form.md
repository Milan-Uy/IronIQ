## High-Bar (Olympic) Squat Technique

The high-bar squat places the barbell on top of the upper traps, directly across the top of the shoulders. This higher bar position allows — and in fact requires — a more upright torso compared to the low-bar squat. The result is a movement that emphasizes the quadriceps more and the posterior chain somewhat less, making it the squat variant preferred in Olympic weightlifting and by many bodybuilders.

### Bar Position

Rest the bar on the upper trapezius, centered across both sides. The bar should sit in a stable groove with the upper traps providing a muscular shelf. Hands grip the bar outside the shoulders; the exact width depends on shoulder mobility, but the goal is a tight upper back. Do not let the wrists bend backward excessively — keep them as neutral as possible.

### Stance and Foot Position

Stance is typically slightly narrower than in low-bar, with toes pointed out 20–35 degrees. The more upright torso mechanics allow this narrower stance without the hips needing to travel as far back. Some lifters do use a wide stance for high-bar, but a moderate-width stance is standard.

### Torso Angle

This is the key difference from low-bar. Because the bar is higher on the back, the moment arm from the bar to the hips is shorter, and therefore less forward lean is needed to keep the bar over the mid-foot. The torso stays more vertical — though "upright" is relative, as some forward lean always occurs. Attempting to keep the torso too vertical while losing depth or balance is a mistake; let the natural torso angle emerge from proper depth and balance.

### Depth

Same standard as low-bar: hip crease below the top of the kneecap. The more upright torso and higher knee travel makes achieving full depth more demanding on ankle mobility. Tight ankles are a common limiter for high-bar squats; elevating the heels on small plates or wearing weightlifting shoes (which have a raised heel) is a legitimate solution.

### Knee Travel

Knees travel further forward over the toes compared to low-bar. This is not a problem — knee travel over the toes is not inherently dangerous for healthy knees. Drive knees out over the toes throughout the squat.

### Key Differences vs. Low-Bar

| Feature | High-Bar | Low-Bar |
|---|---|---|
| Bar placement | Upper traps | Rear delts |
| Torso lean | More upright | More forward |
| Quad emphasis | Higher | Moderate |
| Hip/posterior emphasis | Moderate | Higher |
| Ankle mobility demand | Higher | Lower |

### Common Errors

- **Bar sliding up off the traps:** The bar should not ride the neck. Reposition lower on the traps.
- **Good-morning squat:** Hips rising faster than shoulders on the ascent — cue "chest up."
- **Knees collapsing inward:** Push knees out, especially out of the hole.
- **Insufficient depth:** Ankle stiffness is often the culprit; address with mobility work or heel elevation.
- **Forward lean reverting to low-bar mechanics:** If your torso is nearly horizontal with a high-bar position, the load may be too heavy or technique needs reinforcement.
