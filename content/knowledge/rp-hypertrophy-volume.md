## Renaissance Periodization Volume Landmarks for Hypertrophy

Renaissance Periodization (RP), developed by Dr. Mike Israetel and colleagues, provides a framework for quantifying training volume using three key landmarks: MEV, MAV, and MRV. Understanding and applying these landmarks lets you structure mesocycles that accumulate productive volume while avoiding overtraining.

### The Three Volume Landmarks

**MEV — Minimum Effective Volume**

The lowest volume at which you will still make gains. Below MEV, maintenance or detraining occurs. MEV is different for each muscle group and each individual, but typical estimates:
- Calves, abs: 8–10 sets/week
- Biceps, triceps, lateral deltoids: 6–10 sets/week
- Back, chest, quads: 8–12 sets/week

MEV is useful for deload weeks or when overall stress is high — you can cut to MEV and still maintain most adaptations.

**MAV — Maximum Adaptive Volume**

The range of volume where you make the most gains. Within MAV, more volume produces better results, but you're still recovering adequately. Most of a productive mesocycle should be spent within MAV. MAV is a range, not a single number:
- Most muscle groups: roughly 12–20 working sets per week (highly individual)
- MAV rises as training experience increases and recovery capacity improves

**MRV — Maximum Recoverable Volume**

The maximum volume from which you can fully recover before the next session. Training beyond MRV leads to accumulated fatigue, performance degradation, and ultimately overreaching or overtraining. MRV is not a ceiling to target — it is a ceiling to avoid. Signs you have exceeded MRV: persistent soreness that does not resolve, declining performance week-over-week, poor sleep, elevated resting heart rate.

### Volume Accumulation Within a Mesocycle

A well-structured hypertrophy mesocycle (typically 4–6 weeks) looks like this:

- **Week 1:** Start near MEV for the muscle groups being trained. This allows good performance and minimizes early-phase fatigue.
- **Weeks 2–4:** Progressively add 1–2 sets per muscle group per week. Volume climbs from MEV through MAV.
- **Week 5–6 (if running a longer mesocycle):** Volume may approach MRV. Performance may start to decline. Fatigue accumulates but gains are still being made.
- **Deload week:** Reduce volume dramatically (back to MEV or below) to allow full recovery. Supercompensation occurs during this phase — you may actually feel stronger and see the results of the accumulated work.

### Why Not Just Train at MRV All the Time?

Training at maximum volume sounds like the fastest path to gains, but it is not sustainable. Recovery has a ceiling. Continuously training at or above MRV without deloads leads to diminishing returns, injury risk, and systemic fatigue that impairs all other aspects of performance. The mesocycle structure — build, then recover — is more effective than a constant-high-volume approach.

### Individual Variation

These landmarks are highly individual. Beginners have low MRV (they can't handle much volume before overreaching) but also low MEV (they grow from almost any volume). Advanced lifters have high MRV and high MEV — they need more volume to grow but can also tolerate more.

Genetics, sleep, nutrition, stress levels, age, and training history all affect where your personal landmarks fall. Rather than treating RP's published numbers as exact prescriptions, treat them as starting points and adjust based on your own response: performance trends, recovery quality, and week-to-week strength in the gym.

### Practical Application in IronIQ

When planning your program week-to-week:
- Track total sets per muscle group per week
- Start conservatively (near MEV) at the start of a training block
- Add sets progressively over the mesocycle
- Deload every 4–6 weeks (or when performance declines)
- After the deload, reassess your MEV/MRV for the next block based on how you responded
