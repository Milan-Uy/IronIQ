## Jim Wendler's 5/3/1 Program Overview

5/3/1 is one of the most widely used intermediate-to-advanced strength programs. Created by Jim Wendler (a former elite powerlifter), it is built on slow, conservative progression using sub-maximal training weights, monthly wave cycles, and an emphasis on long-term consistency over short-term maxes.

### The Core Concept: Training Max (TM)

5/3/1 does not use your actual 1RM for calculations. It uses a **Training Max (TM) = 90% of your actual 1RM**. This deliberate reduction means the prescribed percentages produce manageable working weights, reducing the temptation to go too heavy and allowing the "plus sets" to produce legitimate effort without grinding near-max singles.

### The Four-Week Wave

The program runs in four-week cycles. Each week has prescribed percentages based on the TM:

| Week | Set 1 | Set 2 | Set 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Week 1 | 65% × 5 | 75% × 5 | 85% × 5+ |
| Week 2 | 70% × 3 | 80% × 3 | 90% × 3+ |
| Week 3 | 75% × 5 | 85% × 3 | 95% × 1+ |
| Week 4 (Deload) | 40% × 5 | 50% × 5 | 60% × 5 |

The "+" sets are AMRAP (as many reps as possible) — do as many reps as you can with good form. These plus sets are where you gauge your progress and provide the program's primary intensity driver.

### Four Main Lifts, Four Days

5/3/1 is organized around four compound lifts, one per session:
1. Squat
2. Bench Press
3. Deadlift
4. Overhead Press

These can be trained 3 or 4 days per week. Each session focuses on one main lift with its prescribed 5/3/1 sets, followed by assistance work.

### Assistance Work Templates

Several assistance templates exist. Two of the most popular:

**BBB (Boring But Big):** After the main lift sets, do 5 sets × 10 reps of the same lift (or a variation) at 50–60% of TM. High volume, builds hypertrophy alongside strength.

**FSL (First Set Last):** After main sets, return to the first set's weight and do 5×5 or 3×5 at that load. Less volume than BBB, more manageable for lifters adding other accessories.

Both templates are followed by 2–3 accessory exercises for the session's muscle groups: dips, pull-ups, rows, curls, face pulls, etc.

### TM Progression

After each 4-week cycle, increase the TM:
- Upper body lifts (bench, press): +5 lbs
- Lower body lifts (squat, deadlift): +10 lbs

These small increments compound over time. Over a year of consistent training, a lifter adds 60 lbs to their squat and deadlift TM and 30 lbs to their press and bench TM — which translates to meaningful total strength gains without burning out.

### PR Sets and the "1+" Week

Week 3's top set (95% × 1+) is a meaningful PR opportunity. If you hit 5+ reps on a weight you previously hit for 3, that is a training PR. Track these. They provide motivation and confirm the program is working. Do not sandbag plus sets — they are the performance signal in the system.

### Who 5/3/1 Is For

5/3/1 suits intermediate lifters who have exhausted linear progression and need a weekly (rather than session-to-session) progression model. It also works for advanced lifters who value structure, longevity, and the flexibility to add significant assistance work around the main lifts. It is not suited for absolute beginners (LP is faster for them) or for pure powerlifting peaking (more specialized peaking templates exist).

### Common Mistakes

- **Setting TM too high:** The most frequent error. If your plus sets produce fewer reps than expected, reduce the TM by 10% and rebuild.
- **Skipping the deload:** Week 4 is a deload by design. Do not replace it with heavier work.
- **Ignoring assistance:** The main sets alone are insufficient volume for hypertrophy. The assistance work is important.
- **Chasing the plus sets too aggressively:** Rep out to technical failure, not absolute failure. Leave 1 rep in reserve.
