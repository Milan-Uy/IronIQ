## Bench Press Technique

The bench press is the primary upper-body pressing movement in most strength programs. Done correctly, it's a full-body lift — the legs, back, and shoulders all contribute to a stable base from which the chest, shoulders, and triceps press the load.

### Scapular Setup

Before lying down, retract and depress the scapulae: pull the shoulder blades together and down toward the back pockets. This creates a stable, tight upper back and slightly arches the thoracic spine. Maintain this position throughout the set. Scapular instability is the most common cause of shoulder pain in benching — the shoulder joint cannot move efficiently when the scapula is floating loose.

### Arch and Back Position

A natural arch in the lower back is acceptable and beneficial — it shortens the range of motion slightly, brings the chest up toward the bar, and increases stability. Powerlifting exaggerated arches (where only the shoulders and glutes touch the bench) are a specialized technique; for general purposes, a moderate arch where the lower back is off the bench by a fist-width or less is fine and not harmful.

### Foot Position and Leg Drive

Plant the feet flat on the floor (or on the bench for those with short legs) and drive them into the ground during the press. This leg drive is transmitted through the body into the bench and helps stabilize the entire torso. "Leg drive" does not mean pushing yourself up the bench — it means creating full-body tension and a stable base.

### Grip Width

Grip the bar so that the forearms are vertical or very slightly angled inward at the bottom of the lift — this is typically 1.5–2x shoulder width. Measure by marking where the bar sits in the bottom position: the wrists should be directly above the elbows. A grip that's too wide shortens the range of motion but stresses the shoulder joints; too narrow becomes a close-grip bench (a different exercise).

### Bar Path

The bar does not travel straight up and down. It follows a slight arc: the bar is unracked over the upper chest/lower neck, lowered in a slight diagonal to touch the chest at the lower pec line (nipple level or below), then pressed back up and slightly toward the rack to the lockout position over the lower chest. This arc keeps the bar over the shoulder joint's axis of rotation and makes the pressing mechanics more efficient.

### Unracking

With the scapulae already set, lift the bar off the hooks and move it forward and down into the starting position over the chest — do not press it straight up and then lower it. Have a spotter hand you the bar when possible to keep the shoulder position intact.

### Lockout

At the top, arms are extended but the elbows are not hyperextended. The bar is in balance over the chest, not drifting over the face or the belly. Squeeze the chest hard at lockout.

### Common Errors

- **Elbows flared 90 degrees:** Increases shoulder impingement risk; tuck elbows to roughly 45–75 degrees from the torso.
- **Bouncing the bar off the chest:** Reduces muscle tension and risks injury; control the descent.
- **Lifting the hips off the bench:** Converts the bench to an incline and may indicate the load is too heavy.
- **Losing scapular position:** If the shoulders round forward at any point, the lift breaks down.
- **Wrist breakdown:** Bar should be in the palm, not rolled to the fingers — bent wrists under heavy load can cause injury.
