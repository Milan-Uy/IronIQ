## Sumo Deadlift Technique

The sumo deadlift uses a wide stance with the hands gripping the bar inside the legs. This setup reduces the range of motion compared to conventional, shortens the torso's moment arm, and shifts more work onto the hips, adductors, and quads — at the cost of requiring substantial hip external rotation and mobility.

### Stance Width

Set up with a wide stance — feet placed so that when you drop into the starting position, your shins are close to vertical and your torso is upright or only slightly inclined. The exact width varies by individual hip anatomy; some lifters pull with feet nearly touching the plates, others use a moderate-wide stance. A good starting point: step out until your shins form roughly a 70–80 degree angle with the floor when gripped.

Toes point out significantly — 30–60 degrees — in the direction of the knees. The knees must track over the toes throughout the lift; this requires active hip external rotation, not just a wide stance.

### Hip External Rotation

The setup demands that you drive the knees outward and "spread the floor" with your feet — not by pushing the feet outward, but by creating external rotational force through the hips. This activates the hip abductors and external rotators (glutes, piriformis) and allows the hips to drop between the legs rather than behind the bar.

### Starting Position

Because of the wide stance, the hips are lower and closer to the bar at the start than in conventional. The torso is more upright. Grip the bar with hands inside the knees, roughly shoulder-width or slightly narrower. The arms hang straight down. The shins should be close to vertical.

Before pulling: take a full breath, brace the trunk, get your chest up, and "break the floor apart" with your feet to engage the hips.

### The Pull

Initiate by pushing the floor away with the legs — sumo has a more quad-dominant start than conventional. As the bar rises, the torso remains more upright. Drive the hips through at lockout — hips and shoulders rise at the same rate. Lockout is the same as conventional: stand tall, hips fully extended, glutes squeezed.

### Key Differences vs. Conventional

| Feature | Sumo | Conventional |
|---|---|---|
| Stance | Wide | Narrow to moderate |
| Torso angle | More upright | More inclined |
| Range of motion | Shorter | Longer |
| Hip/adductor demand | Higher | Lower |
| Lower back demand | Lower | Higher |
| Mobility requirement | Hip external rotation | Hamstring flexibility |

### Who Benefits from Sumo

Lifters with long torsos relative to leg length often find sumo more mechanically efficient. Those with wide hips and good hip external rotation mobility are well-suited to sumo. However, sumo requires significant time to develop the hip mobility and motor pattern — switching from conventional and expecting immediate gains is unrealistic.

### Common Errors

- **Knees caving inward:** The single most common sumo error. Drive knees out over toes aggressively from the start.
- **Hips too high at setup:** Converts the lift to a stiff-leg sumo with excessive lower back demand. Sit the hips down into the start position.
- **Hips rising faster than shoulders:** Same as conventional — chest up cue, push the floor away.
- **Bar drifting forward:** Keep the bar on the body; active lats throughout.
- **Insufficient hip lock at lockout:** Drive hips through fully; a half-lockout is a failed lift in competition and leaves the glutes undertrained.
