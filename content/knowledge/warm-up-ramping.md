## Warm-Up and Ramping to Working Sets

A proper warm-up serves two purposes: preparing the body for heavy work (raising core temperature, improving joint lubrication, activating target muscles) and ramping up to the working weight so that the first working set is not a shock to the system. Skipping or rushing warm-ups increases injury risk and reduces performance on the working sets.

### General vs. Specific Warm-Up

**General warm-up (5–10 minutes):** Raises core body temperature and increases blood flow to working muscles. Options: rowing machine, light cycling, jump rope, brisk walking. This is especially important in cold environments or first thing in the morning when body temperature is lower. If you are already warm from previous sets in the same session (e.g., squats after a deadlift warm-up), you may skip the general warm-up for subsequent exercises.

**Specific warm-up (ramping sets):** Sets of the main lift using progressively heavier loads, working up to the working weight. These activate the specific motor patterns, joint angles, and muscles involved in the movement. Ramping sets should be at full speed and with perfect technique — they are not "practice reps," they are preparation.

### How Many Ramp Sets?

The number of warm-up sets scales with the working weight. Light working sets require fewer ramp sets; heavy working sets require more:

**General guideline for a 200 lb working set (example: squat 3×5 @ 200 lbs):**

| Set | Load | Reps |
|---|---|---|
| Warm-up 1 | 45 lb (bar only) | 5 |
| Warm-up 2 | 95 lb | 5 |
| Warm-up 3 | 135 lb | 3 |
| Warm-up 4 | 165 lb | 2 |
| Warm-up 5 | 185 lb | 1 |
| Working Set 1 | 200 lb | 5 |

**General guideline for a 400 lb working set:**

| Set | Load | Reps |
|---|---|---|
| Warm-up 1 | 45 lb | 5 |
| Warm-up 2 | 135 lb | 5 |
| Warm-up 3 | 225 lb | 3 |
| Warm-up 4 | 295 lb | 2 |
| Warm-up 5 | 345 lb | 1 |
| Warm-up 6 | 385 lb | 1 |
| Working Set 1 | 400 lb | — |

The ramp should make large jumps early (when the loads are light and the jumps are easy) and smaller jumps near the working weight to dial in the feel of the weight. The last warm-up set should be within 10–15% of the working weight.

### Reps on Warm-Up Sets

Keep warm-up reps low, especially as you approach the working weight. High reps at 80–90% of working weight accumulate fatigue that degrades the quality of the actual working sets. Singles or doubles are appropriate at 90%+ of working weight. The bar feels heavy enough without doing five reps when you are 90% of the way to working weight.

### Accessory Exercise Warm-Ups

Most accessory lifts (curls, lateral raises, cable rows, etc.) require minimal ramping — one or two light feeder sets is sufficient. Only the primary compound lifts typically require extended ramping. If training in a full session, later exercises benefit from the warm-up accumulated during earlier exercises.

### Between-Set Rest During Ramp

Rest minimally between warm-up sets — 60–90 seconds is enough. Save longer rest periods for the working sets. The warm-up phase should be efficient, not a 20-minute ordeal.

### Mobility Work as Part of Warm-Up

If you have known mobility restrictions (tight hips for squats, tight pecs for bench, limited thoracic extension for overhead work), address them before the specific warm-up with 2–5 minutes of targeted mobility work. This is not a stretch-for-30-minutes routine — brief, targeted work on known restrictions is all that is needed.

### Cold Environments and Morning Sessions

In cold gym conditions or early-morning sessions when body temperature has not risen, spend an additional 5 minutes on the general warm-up. Muscles and connective tissue are stiffer and more injury-prone when cold. The ramp may also need to start lighter and progress more gradually.

### Common Errors

- **Skipping warm-ups entirely:** Especially risky with heavy compound lifts. Connective tissue (tendons, ligaments) warms up more slowly than muscles and requires progressive loading.
- **Too many reps on heavy warm-up sets:** Fatigues muscles before working sets begin.
- **Resting too long between warm-up sets:** Reduces the efficiency of the session; warm-ups should flow.
- **Identical warm-up regardless of working weight:** Ramping to a 135 lb working set requires far less warm-up than ramping to a 400 lb set.
