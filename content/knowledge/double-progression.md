## Double Progression: Rep-Then-Weight Method

Double progression is the primary progression model for intermediate and advanced lifters in hypertrophy-focused programs. Unlike linear progression (where load increases every session), double progression first fills a rep range with good-form reps, then increases the load. The "double" refers to progressing two variables: reps first, then weight.

### How It Works

You are given a rep range target, such as 3×8-12. You start a weight where you can complete all sets but not reach the top of the range. As sessions pass, you accumulate reps. When you can complete all sets at the top of the rep range, you increase the load and drop back to the bottom of the range.

**Example — Incline Dumbbell Press, 3×8-12:**

| Week | Set 1 | Set 2 | Set 3 | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 8 | 7 | 6 | Continue, accumulate reps |
| 2 | 10 | 9 | 8 | Continue |
| 3 | 12 | 11 | 10 | Continue |
| 4 | 12 | 12 | 12 | Increase load by 5 lbs, reset to 8 reps |
| 5 | 9 | 8 | 7 | Begin new cycle |

### Why It Works

Double progression respects the reality that intermediate lifters cannot add weight every session. Their recovery cycle and adaptation window are longer — a week or more. By working within a rep range, the lifter is automatically auto-regulating: on a good day, they hit the top of the range; on a fatigued day, they stay in the middle. This self-corrects without external tracking of readiness.

Additionally, spending multiple weeks at a given load accumulates volume at that intensity, which is the primary driver of hypertrophy for intermediate trainees. The rep range ensures the work is heavy enough to drive adaptation but not so heavy that recovery is overwhelmed.

### Choosing Rep Ranges

The rep range should match the training goal:
- **Strength-focused hypertrophy:** 3×5-8 (heavier loads, more CNS demand)
- **Standard hypertrophy:** 3×8-12 or 4×8-12 (the most common range for muscle growth)
- **Higher rep hypertrophy/pump work:** 3×12-20 (accessory work, isolation movements)

Smaller muscles and isolation exercises (curls, lateral raises) typically use higher rep ranges than compound movements.

### Load Increment Size

When adding load after filling the rep range:
- Barbells: 5 lbs for upper body, 10 lbs for lower body
- Dumbbells: 5 lbs (2.5 per dumbbell) for upper body — using fractional plates or microloading handles
- Cables: 5–10 lbs depending on the machine's weight stack resolution

If the increment is too large (you drop to the very bottom of the rep range or below), it indicates the jump was too aggressive. Consider smaller microloads.

### Set Variations

Double progression can apply to any set scheme:
- **3 sets:** Standard; easy to track
- **4–5 sets:** More volume per session; each set has its own rep count in the range
- **Top set + back-off sets:** Fill the top set's rep range, keep back-off sets at 70–80% of top set load

### When Double Progression Is No Longer Enough

Advanced lifters who stall on double progression for multiple mesocycles may need to move to periodized loading (wave loading, block periodization) where the rep range itself changes across planned training phases rather than within a single range indefinitely.

### Common Mistakes

- **Increasing load before filling the rep range:** Patience is required; do not jump the progression.
- **Rep ranges too narrow:** A 3-rep range (e.g., 10-12) fills too quickly and creates large load jumps; 4-5 reps of range (8-12) gives more room.
- **Ignoring form degradation at the top of the range:** All reps must be technically sound — grinding out two final reps with breakdown does not count as "filling the range."
