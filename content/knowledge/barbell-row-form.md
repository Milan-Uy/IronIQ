## Barbell Row Technique

The barbell row is the primary horizontal pulling movement used in most strength programs. It builds the lats, rhomboids, rear deltoids, and mid-traps, while also developing the ability to hold a braced, hinged position under load — making it useful for deadlift and squat strength as well.

### Setup and Hip Hinge

Stand over the bar with a conventional deadlift-like stance. Hinge at the hips until the torso is at roughly 30–45 degrees from horizontal (some programs use a more horizontal "Pendlay" style, others allow a more upright torso). The exact torso angle depends on the style being used:

- **Barbell Row (Bent-Over Row):** Torso at 45 degrees, bar pulled to the upper abdomen or lower sternum. The hips can extend slightly during the pull (some body English allowed).
- **Pendlay Row:** Torso nearly parallel to the floor, bar starts from the floor each rep, pulled explosively to the lower chest, and returned to the floor under control. Strict, heavy, and excellent for upper back power.

Brace the trunk and maintain a neutral spine throughout. The lower back should not round — if it does, the load is too heavy or the hinge pattern is not established.

### Grip Variants

- **Pronated (overhand) grip:** More rhomboid and mid-trap activation; harder on the elbows and wrists for some. The elbows flare out at roughly 45–70 degrees from the torso.
- **Supinated (underhand) grip:** More bicep involvement; elbows stay tucked closer to the body; often allows a stronger pull for beginners. Feels more like a standing curl combined with a back movement.

Both grips are valid. Choose based on which allows better lat engagement and elbow comfort.

### Pull Direction

- **Pull to the belly/lower abdomen:** Elbows angle back and tight to the sides; targets the lats more directly.
- **Pull to the chest/lower sternum:** Elbows flare out more; targets the upper back and rear deltoids more.

Neither is wrong — they train slightly different portions of the pulling musculature. Most lifters benefit from the belly pull for lat thickness and the chest pull for upper back development.

### Lat Engagement and Scapular Motion

Initiate the row by depressing the scapulae (pulling the shoulder blades down) before pulling the elbows back. This "pre-sets" the lats. At the top of the row, squeeze the shoulder blades together (retraction). The scapulae should go through full retraction and protraction each rep for maximum muscle involvement — avoid simply moving the arms without moving the shoulder blades.

### Full Range of Motion

Allow the arms to fully extend at the bottom of each rep — do not bounce the bar or keep constant tension with a short range. The stretch at the bottom loads the lats through their full length. Controlled descent is part of the work.

### Body English and Cheating

A small amount of hip extension (hip drive) on heavy rows is normal and acceptable, particularly in the barbell row (not Pendlay). This is similar to how a slight body swing on a chin-up is acceptable at heavier loads. However, converting the row into a shrug-then-pull where the torso becomes nearly vertical defeats the purpose.

### Common Errors

- **Lower back rounding:** Insufficient bracing or too much load. Reduce weight and focus on the hinge position.
- **Pulling with the biceps only:** Arms curling rather than elbows driving back with lat engagement. Cue: drive elbows back, not hands up.
- **Short range of motion:** Not allowing full arm extension at the bottom; the lat is not loaded through its full range.
- **Torso bouncing excessively:** Moderate body English is fine; but if the torso swings 30+ degrees each rep, load is too heavy for the intended stimulus.
- **Bar drifting away from the body:** The bar should move in a nearly vertical path below the torso, close to the thighs.
