## Overhead Press (Barbell) Technique

The barbell overhead press — also called the strict press or military press — is the fundamental vertical pressing movement. It builds the deltoids, triceps, and upper back while demanding full-body tension and balance. Unlike the bench press, there is no bench to stabilize you; the entire body must act as a rigid base.

### Grip and Starting Position

Grip the bar with the bar resting in the heel of the palm — the same palm-over-fingers contact as the bench press. The grip is just outside shoulder width so that the forearms are vertical (or very close to it) when the bar is at the start position at the clavicle and top of the chest.

The bar begins at the front of the shoulders — in the rack, it should be at approximately upper-chest height. Unrack by pushing up and stepping back one step. The bar rests against the throat and upper chest, or as close to it as anatomy allows.

### Stance and Full-Body Tension

Stand with feet roughly hip-width apart. Before pressing, take a breath and brace hard — the entire torso, glutes, and legs should be under tension. The press is pressed with the entire body locked; a loose lower body results in an inefficient transfer of force and invites lower back hyperextension.

Squeeze the glutes. This is the single most underrated cue in the overhead press — it locks the pelvis in a neutral position and prevents the common error of leaning the lower back back to compensate for shoulder or thoracic stiffness.

### Bar Path Past the Face

The bar does not travel straight up from the front of the shoulders. Instead, the bar moves back and up: the head and neck move backward (not the bar forward) to clear the face, the bar passes the face in a close arc, and then the head moves forward under the bar as it passes the crown of the head. At lockout, the bar is directly above the shoulder joint (and the mid-foot), with the head forward through the "window" created by the arms.

This bar path keeps the bar over the body's center of mass throughout the movement. Pressing forward in front of the face — keeping the face in the way — creates an inefficient moment arm and stresses the shoulder joints.

### Lockout

At the top: arms fully extended, elbows locked, bar over the mid-foot (viewed from the side), head through. The upper traps should be actively shrugged up into the bar at lockout — think of "finishing the press" by shrugging at the top. This is not cheating; it is correct mechanics that stabilizes the shoulder girdle at end range.

### The Descent

Lower the bar with control back to the starting position at the clavicle. The same bar arc in reverse — head moves back as the bar passes the face, head moves forward once the bar is below the chin level.

### Common Errors

- **Leaning back excessively:** Creates a quasi-incline press and risks lower back strain. Glutes squeezed, neutral pelvis. If you cannot press without leaning back significantly, the load is too heavy or thoracic mobility is insufficient.
- **Bar forward of the body at lockout:** Keeps the center of mass in front of the base; the bar must finish directly over the mid-foot and hips.
- **Not clearing the face:** Pressing around the face rather than moving the head back results in a longer, less efficient bar path.
- **Pressing with a loose base:** Any movement in the hips or feet dissipates force. Lock everything down.
- **Not shrugging at lockout:** The shoulder blades should not remain retracted at the top — they should elevate with the arms to allow full overhead position.
