## Pull-Up and Chin-Up Technique

Pull-ups and chin-ups are foundational vertical pulling movements that require no barbell — just a bar and the ability to lift bodyweight. They build the lats, biceps, and upper back, and because they're calisthenic movements, they also develop body control and core stability throughout the movement.

### Grip Variants

- **Pull-up (overhand/pronated grip):** Hands face away from you, typically shoulder-width to slightly wider. Emphasizes the lats and upper back; the biceps contribute but in a mechanically disadvantaged position. Generally harder than chin-ups.
- **Chin-up (underhand/supinated grip):** Hands face toward you, roughly shoulder-width. Greater bicep involvement due to mechanical advantage; many find them easier. Also strong lat recruitment.
- **Neutral grip:** Palms face each other (requires a neutral grip bar or rings). Elbow-friendly, strong lat and brachialis activation, excellent option for those with wrist or elbow issues.

### Scapular Initiation

The most important cue: initiate every rep by depressing and retracting the scapulae before bending the elbows. Pull the shoulder blades down and together ("put your shoulder blades in your back pockets") before the arms begin pulling. This activates the lats correctly, protects the shoulder joint from impingement, and prevents the common error of pulling with the arms alone while the shoulders shrug up toward the ears.

Dead hangs at the bottom of each rep allow the scapulae to fully elevate and protract — this full range of motion maximizes lat stretch and development.

### Range of Motion

A full rep: start from a dead hang (arms fully extended, shoulders relaxed or actively depressed), pull until the chin clears the bar (for pull-ups/chin-ups) or the chest reaches bar height (for chest-to-bar pull-ups). Do not stop short of chin-over-bar — partial reps train a shorter range and disguise weakness at the top. Return to a full dead hang at the bottom.

### Body Position

Keep the body relatively straight or in a slight hollow body position — core braced, hips and legs hanging below the bar without excessive swinging. Some anterior pelvic tilt and leg swing develops under load, but significant kipping (using momentum from the hips) converts it to a different skill (a kipping pull-up used in CrossFit, which has its own applications).

### Assistance Progressions (for those who cannot yet do unassisted reps)

- **Band-assisted pull-ups:** A resistance band looped around the bar and under the feet or knees offloads a portion of bodyweight. Allows full range of motion practice.
- **Lat pulldown:** Machine equivalent; allows load adjustment and good lat training while building toward unassisted pull-ups.
- **Negative (eccentric) pull-ups:** Jump or step to the top position and lower yourself as slowly as possible (3–5 seconds). Eccentric strength builds quickly and transfers to the concentric.
- **Australian pull-ups (bodyweight rows):** Horizontal pulling with feet on the floor; reduces the load and builds pulling strength.

### Weighted Progressions

Once bodyweight pull-ups are achievable for multiple sets of 8–10 reps, add load via a dipping belt, a weight vest, or a dumbbell held between the feet. Progress load as you would a barbell lift — add small increments when the top of the rep range is reached comfortably across sets.

### Common Errors

- **Pulling with shoulders shrugged:** Shoulder blades should depress, not elevate. Active scapular depression throughout.
- **Partial range of motion:** Stopping below chin-over-bar at the top, or not reaching a full dead hang at the bottom.
- **Excessive kipping:** In a strict training context, kipping bypasses the strength requirements that make the exercise valuable.
- **Head jutting forward:** "Chicken-necking" at the top to get the chin over the bar — the chin should clear the bar through pulling strength, not head position trickery.
- **Grip width too wide:** Extremely wide grips shorten the lat's range of motion and are harder on the shoulders; shoulder-width to slightly wider is sufficient.
